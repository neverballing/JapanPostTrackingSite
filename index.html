Information (How to read graphs)

1 dot = 1 package

The X axis represents the date that a package became stuck in the departure office (likely OSAKA INT).

The Y axis represents the time it took for a package to become unstuck, i.e. resurface in the destination country and be scanned by its post service.

The red line represents the present (now).

In theory, a package that is scanned by the destination country today would appear somewhere on the red line.

Thus, in theory, it should be impossible for data points to appear right of the red line.

However, in practice, due to the way data is rendered on the graph without regard for timezones, packages may appear right of the red line despite this.

FAQ

What's the point of all this?

The point is to provide historical statistics on Japan Post small package airmail shipments out of the country. Although these packages are airmail, they rely on extra unused space on passenger flights to keep the cost down. Because of this, packages are known to sometimes get stuck or backlogged in their origin country waiting for a flight. Because of this, there can be a long queue of packages waiting to be shipped. Most delivery estimator tools do not account for this variance and estimate a single shipping time, when in reality it depends on where you (the package recipient) live. Therefore, you can identify package delays and come up with a more accurate delivery estimate, and make an informed decision whether to use small package airmail or not.

Is my country's small package airmail situation normal/abnormal?

Look at the graph, and compare what's close to the red line to the past. If points close to present are much higher than the past, packages may be delayed. Mexico's graphs are a great example of delays being obvious on the graph. In the future I'd like to add some sort of automatic indicator, but considering statistical variance of packages, certainty of predictions, accounting for what's "normal" would be difficult.

Does processing facility matter?

Most countries have one processing facility. A few big countries like the United States and China have multiple. Flights go from Japan to the processing facility, and each processing facility has its own queue. As a result, delays can sometimes affect one processing facility but not the others.

I live in the United States, which one is my processing facility?

P&DC HONOLULU HI 968 (USPS) services Hawaii, and maybe the island territories, I'm not sure about that second part yet. ISC LOS ANGELES CA (USPS) services all of California. And, everyone else is serviced by ISC NEW YORK NY (USPS). As of the writing of this, ISC New York packages are kinda slow (~10 days stalled) whereas the others are fast. Lucky you, if you live in California or Hawaii.

About those graphs... Is the Y-axis the delivery time for the package?

The Y-axis is just the time the package was stalled. That is strictly from the time the package started waiting in line to the time the destination country acknowledged that the package has entered the country (at the processing facility). So, the time it takes to get from the processing facility to the recipient's doorstep is not counted, and the time it takes from the sender to the waiting line is not counted. I collected data on exact delivery times but I haven't done anything with it yet. If you know approximately how long it takes for packages to move around within your country, add that to the estimated stall time, then add about 1 more day for movement time within Japan and to account for time zones and such.

I live in Canada or Australia.

These countries do not track their small package airmails once they enter the country. They are totally untracked, the countries don't even acknowledge it has arrived. For that reason I have absolutely no data on how long packages are stalled from these countries, sorry.

Why don't the percents add to 100%?

The packages that are still stalled are included with the total and can be attributed to the destination country, but their target processing facility is not yet known, so they are not attributed to any specific processing facility. If your country's only processing facility is very different from 100%, it may only be tracking packages sporadically, in which case the untracked packages are considered the same as stalled forever (like Canada and Australia).

Packages that got returned to sender for one reason or another ended up as country Kanagawa in my data because they were tracked as they re-entered Japan. I left it in there for the lolz.
